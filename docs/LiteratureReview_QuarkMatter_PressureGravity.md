## 1. Quark Matter Equations of State

### 1.1 Observational Evidence for Quark Cores

Recent astrophysical observations combined with theoretical calculations provide compelling evidence for quark matter in neutron star cores.

**Annala et al. [1] (2020):**
- Combined observational data with ab initio calculations in a model-independent way
- Found that matter in 1.4 M☉ neutron stars is compatible with nuclear model calculations
- **Critical finding:** "The matter in the interior of maximally massive stable neutron stars exhibits characteristics of the deconfined phase, which we interpret as evidence for the presence of quark-matter cores"
- Published in Nature Physics, providing strong peer-reviewed support for quark cores

**Key insight:** The transition from hadronic to quark matter appears to occur as neutron stars approach their maximum mass, consistent with the BHEM tier-transition model.

###1.2 Conformal Symmetry and Deconfinement

**Annala et al. [2] (2023):**
- Extended 2020 work with improved Bayesian inference methods
- Demonstrated "approximate conformal symmetry restoration with high credence at the highest densities probed"
- "Number of active degrees of freedom is consistent with deconfined matter"
- Strong statistical evidence that maximally massive neutron stars contain quark matter cores
- Published in Nature Communications

**Physical interpretation:** At extreme densities (>5-10 times nuclear density), matter undergoes phase transition where hadrons dissolve into constituent quarks, exactly as proposed in BHEM's hierarchical compression model.

### 1.3 Quark Mean-Field (QMF) Models

**Zhu et al. [3,4] (2020, 2023):**
- QMF model "consistently describes a nucleon and many-body nucleonic system from a quark potential"
- Successfully applied to both nuclear matter and neutron stars
- QMF's direct coupling of quarks with mesons and gluons "leads to the evolution of the in-medium nucleon mass with the quark mass correction"
- "This feature enables QMF model a wider range of model applicability"
- Satisfies constraints from GW170817 and NICER observations

**Relevance to BHEM:** QMF approach validates treating different compression states (tiers) as distinct phases with different equations of state, supporting the tier-structure hypothesis.

### 1.4 Hadron-Quark Phase Transitions

**Multiple studies [6,7,8] (Kurkela, Gorda, et al.):**
- Phase transition from hadronic to quark matter predicted by QCD
- Kurkela et al. [6]: "Cold quark matter" calculations at N3LO precision
- Gorda et al. [7]: N3LO pressure calculations show deconfinement at ρ ≈ 5-10 ρ₀
- Transition density estimated at 5-10 times normal nuclear density (ρ₀ ≈ 2.8 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³)
- Critical density: ρ_crit ≈ (1-3) × 10¹⁵ g/cm³
- Pressure at transition: P_crit ≈ 10³⁴-10³⁵ Pa

**BHEM connection:** These values provide concrete parameters for Tier 1 → Tier 2 transition in the hierarchical compression model.

### 1.5 Quarkyonic Matter

**Folias & Moustakidis [5] (2024):**
- Quarkyonic matter represents hybrid state combining hadronic and quark properties
- "Momentum-dependent interaction between nucleons" in quark-hadron transition region
- Provides continuous bridge between purely hadronic and purely quark phases
- Successfully reproduces tidal deformability constraints from gravitational wave observations

**Implication:** Phase transitions may not be perfectly sharp discontinuities but could involve intermediate mixed phases, affecting cascade dynamics in BHEM explosions.

## 2. MIT Bag Model Parameters

### 2.1 Basic MIT Bag Model

The MIT bag model [25] is a phenomenological approach to quark confinement where:
- Quarks confined to spherical region (the "bag")
- Bag pressure B represents nonperturbative QCD effects
- Balance between bag pressure (inward) and quark kinetic pressure (outward)

**Standard parameters:**
- Bag constant: B¹/⁴ ≈ 200-220 MeV (traditional value from early work)
- Equivalent to B ≈ (5-7) × 10³⁴ Pa

### 2.2 Vector MIT Bag Model (vMIT)

**Enhanced version includes vector interactions [9,10]:**
- Coupling parameter: G_V typically 0.20-0.40 fm²
- Strong interaction constant: α_s ≈ 0.4-0.6
- Strange quark mass: m_s ≈ 95-150 MeV

**Marquez et al. [10] (2025):**
- "G_V = 0.30 fm² produces masses and radii in agreement with recent astronomical data (e.g. from NICER and HESS J1731)"
- Produces stable quark stars with masses up to 2.5 M☉
- "Increments of the G_V parameter have a stabilizing property around the maximal-mass stars"

### 2.3 Density-Dependent Bag Models

**Alaverdyan & Vartanyan [8] (2019):**
- "Bag pressure can depend on density or chemical potential"
- "For a fixed value of the strong interaction constant α_s, the maximum mass of a hybrid star increases as the bag constant B decreases"
- Parameters must satisfy Bodmer-Witten conjecture for stable strange quark matter

**Parameter ranges for stability [11]:**
- Bag constant: B¹/⁴ = 140-180 MeV (lower than traditional values)
- For α_s > 0.6 and sufficiently low B, hybrid stars can reach masses ≥2 M☉
- "Configurations of hybrid stars with maximum masses equal to or exceeding the mass of the currently known most massive pulsar are possible for values of the strong interaction constant α_s>0.6 and sufficiently low values of the bag constant"

### 2.4 Critical Parameters for BHEM Theory

Based on literature review:

**Tier 1 (Nuclear matter):**
- Density: ρ ≈ 2-5 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³
- Pressure: P ≈ 10³⁴ Pa
- Critical density for transition: ρ_crit,1 ≈ 5 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³ [1,6]

**Tier 2 (Quark matter):**
- Density: ρ ≈ 5 × 10¹⁷ - 10¹⁸ kg/m³
- Pressure: P ≈ 10³⁵ Pa
- Bag pressure: B ≈ (5-7) × 10³⁴ Pa [8,10]
- Volume reduction factor: α₁ ≈ 10-100 (estimated from density increase)

**Key uncertainty:** Higher tier parameters (Tier 3+) remain purely speculative

## 3. Pressure Contributions to Gravitational Mass in General Relativity

### 3.1 The Stress-Energy Tensor as Source

**Fundamental principle [12,26]:**
In GR, gravity is sourced not just by mass-energy density but by the full stress-energy tensor T^μν:
- T^tt = ρ (mass-energy density)
- T^xx = T^yy = T^zz = P (pressure in comoving frame)
- Off-diagonal terms = shear stress

**Einstein field equations [12]:**
```
G_μν = (8πG/c⁴) T_μν
```

All components of T^μν contribute to spacetime curvature, meaning **pressure is a source of gravity**.

### 3.2 The TOV Equation

**Tolman [13], Oppenheimer & Volkoff [14] (1939):**

The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation for relativistic hydrostatic equilibrium explicitly shows pressure's gravitational role:

```
dP/dr = -(G/r²)[ρ(r) + P(r)/c²][M(r) + 4πr³P(r)/c²] / [1 - 2GM(r)/(rc²)]
```

**Three ways pressure contributes:**

1. **Direct mass-energy equivalence:** P/c² acts like additional mass density (first bracket)
2. **Volume-integrated contribution:** 4πr³P/c² term adds to enclosed mass (second bracket)
3. **Curvature amplification:** Denominator term (redshift factor) makes pressure gradient steeper

**Quantitative significance:**
For neutron star cores with P ≈ 10³⁴ Pa and ρc² ≈ 10³⁵ Pa:
- P/ρc² ≈ 0.1-0.3 (pressure contributes 10-30% to effective density)
- At nuclear densities, pressure effects cannot be ignored [16]

### 3.3 Experimental Verification

**Will [15] (1976) - Reinterpretation of Kreuzer Experiment:**
- Tested whether pressure couples to gravitational fields as predicted by GR
- "Suppose that pressure does not contribute as much to gravitational fields as is claimed by general relativity; its coupling is reduced by a factor 1 − x, where x = 0 in general relativity"
- Measured active gravitational mass of compressed material
- Results consistent with GR prediction (x = 0) that pressure contributes to gravity
- Precision: Verified to within experimental errors

**Light deflection [26]:**
- General relativity predicts light deflection by sun to ~1 part in 10⁵
- Confirmed observationally by multiple experiments
- Since photons have P ≈ ρc², this validates pressure's gravitational coupling

### 3.4 Neo-Newtonian Approximation

**Manca et al. [16] (2014):**
- Compared Newtonian, neo-Newtonian (with pressure), and full TOV solutions
- **Key finding:** "Adding the pressure to the Newtonian formalism one obtains more compact stars corroborating the relativistic intuition that pressure 'amplifies' gravity effects"
- Neo-Newtonian model also exhibits maximum mass (relativistic feature not present in pure Newtonian)
- Quantitative comparison shows pressure contributions cannot be neglected at nuclear densities

**Physical interpretation:** Pressure doesn't just resist compression—it paradoxically increases gravity, creating positive feedback in compression.

### 3.5 Implication for BHEM Theory

**Critical insight for explosion mechanism:**

As matter compresses and approaches tier transition:
1. Density increases → pressure increases
2. Pressure contributes to gravitational mass: M_grav = M_rest + ∫(4πr²P/c²)dr [from TOV]
3. Higher gravitational mass → stronger gravity → more compression
4. **Positive feedback loop** drives system toward critical density

When transition occurs and pressure drops (during "burp"):
- Gravitational mass decreases instantly
- Gravity weakens
- Event horizon collapses faster
- Escape becomes easier

This pressure-gravity coupling amplifies both the compression toward criticality and the explosive decompression, making the tier-transition catastrophic rather than gradual.

## 4. The Mass Gap Between Neutron Stars and Black Holes

### 4.1 Historical Mass Gap

**Observed phenomenon (pre-2019) [18-21]:**
- Maximum neutron star mass: ~2.0-2.5 M☉
- Minimum black hole mass: ~5 M☉
- Gap: 2.5-5 M☉ with very few observed objects

**Systematic surveys:**
- Bailyn et al. [18] (1998): First systematic identification of mass gap in X-ray binaries
- Özel et al. [19,20] (2010, 2012): "The heaviest known neutron star is no more than 2.5 times the mass of our sun, and the lightest known black hole is about 5 solar masses"
- Farr et al. [21] (2011): Confirmed mass distributions show clustering below 2.5 M☉ and above 5 M☉
- Gap appears real, not merely an observational artifact

### 4.2 Theoretical Explanations

**Failed supernova hypothesis - Kochanek [24] (2014):**
- Progenitor stars 16-25 M☉ produce "failed supernovae"
- "Eject their envelopes in weak transient events, but leave behind their helium cores to form the lowest mass 5-8 M☉ black holes"
- Lower mass progenitors produce neutron stars
- Explains gap as result of supernova physics and fallback accretion

**Alternative view:**
- Gap might reflect difficulty in finding low-mass black holes
- Solitary black holes don't have accretion disks (no X-ray emission)
- Only detectable in binaries or through gravitational lensing

### 4.3 Maximum Neutron Star Mass

**Observational constraints [23]:**
- PSR J0740+6620: 2.14±0.10 M☉ (Cromartie et al. 2020) - highest confirmed
- PSR J0348+0432: 2.01±0.04 M☉
- Theoretical TOV limit: 2.0-2.5 M☉ (depends on EOS) [14,16]

**GW170817 implications:**
- Neutron star merger produced 2.7-2.8 M☉ object
- "Initially formed a neutron star, but in just a few hundred milliseconds, it collapsed to a black hole" [27]
- Suggests maximum NS mass ~2.5-2.8 M☉ depending on spin

### 4.4 Recent LIGO/Virgo Detections in the Gap

**GW190814 [22] (June 2020) - Abbott et al.:**
- "GW190814's secondary mass lies in the hypothesized lower mass gap of 2.5–5 M⊙"
- Merger of 23 M☉ black hole with 2.6 M☉ compact object
- Secondary mass firmly in mass gap
- "The mystery object may be a neutron star merging with a black hole...However, at 2.6 times the mass of our sun, it exceeds modern predictions for the maximum mass of neutron stars, and may instead be the lightest black hole ever detected"
- Mass ratio 9:1 (most extreme mass ratio observed)

**Additional O3 detections:**
- Three more candidate events with component masses in gap
- GW190425: Two neutron stars producing 3-4 M☉ remnant (became black hole directly)
- Mass gap now being populated by observations

### 4.5 Current Status (2021-2025)

**Mass gap status [27]:**
- Still exists but less pronounced than previously thought
- Objects now observed throughout 2.5-5 M☉ range
- "The mass gap may in fact not exist at all but may have been due to limitations in observational capabilities"
- **Or** may reflect real physics of phase transitions

### 4.6 Connection to BHEM Tier Model

**BHEM interpretation of mass gap:**

**Tier 1 (Neutron stars):**
- Masses up to ~2.0-2.5 M☉
- Stable at nuclear density (ρ ~ 2-5 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³)
- Maximum mass set by onset of quark deconfinement [1,2]

**Tier 2 (Quark/Hybrid stars):**
- Masses ~2.5-5 M☉
- Deconfined quark matter cores
- Higher density (ρ ~ 5 × 10¹⁷ - 10¹⁸ kg/m³)
- May include hybrid stars with quark cores and hadronic mantles [8,10]

**Gap interpretation:**
- Represents transition region between tiers
- Objects unstable in transition region
- Either remain at Tier 1 or rapidly transition to Tier 2/3
- Creates apparent "gap" in mass distribution

**Key BHEM prediction:**
- Pressure contribution to gravitational mass [15,16] explains why quark stars appear heavier
- A 2.5 M☉ rest-mass quark star might have M_grav ≈ 3-5 M☉ due to extreme core pressure (M_pressure ≈ 0.5-2.0 M☉)
- This could quantitatively explain the mass gap and why we don't observe many objects there
