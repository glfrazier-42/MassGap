## Opening: The Answer First

There are two mass gaps: 2.5-5.0 M☉ (approximately) and 50-150 M☉ (again, approximately). Stars don't count—we are talking about inactive objects. There have been objects discovered in those gaps, but they are rare. Explanations have been offered for each of the gaps, but they are not wholly satisfactory. In particular, we are offering a single theory that explains both gaps. 

**Core thesis:** Every mass gap marks the point at which gravity causes matter to collapse to the next-finer granularity of state. Per General Relativity, pressure contributes to gravity, and it is the added pressure from the step-function increase in density that causes an *observed* step function in mass; there is no baryonic mass gap. Stellar objects are equally likely to have any amount of baryonic matter in them, and in fact incrementally grow by accreting matter. The gaps exist purely in the observed mass—the amount of gravity generated by the object.

## Section: Upper Mass Gap and Objects Within Gaps

**Extension of the theory:**
We have shown that the 2.5-5.0 M☉ mass gap is due to the collapse of neutrons to quarks.

**Prediction:** The 50-150 M☉ mass gap is due to the collapse of quarks to preons.

**Further prediction:** If we were able to develop a catalog of black holes of arbitrarily large sizes, other mass gaps would be revealed at higher masses.

**Why do we observe objects in mass gaps?**

There are two ways an object can reside in a mass gap:

### Mechanism 1: Spin Support

As shown by the pulsar data, a neutron star that is not spinning will collapse to a quark star (black hole) at less than 1.5 M☉.

**Every pulsar (and, in fact, every neutron star) observed that is greater than 1.5 M☉ exists solely due to its spin.**

The 2.5 M☉ limit on pulsar size marks the maximum rotation speed that a pulsar can have.

**We propose this is true of the 50-150 M☉ gap as well:**
- 50 M☉ marks the point at which the fastest-spinning quark stars collapse to preon stars
- Slower spinning quark stars collapse at much lower masses

### Mechanism 2: Collision-Induced Early Collapse

The aggregate mass at which the next tier of matter (e.g., preons) becomes stable is lower than the aggregate mass at which the current tier of matter (e.g., quarks) becomes unstable.

Given a stable quark star above the threshold at which preon stars become stable: if an event were to cause the core of that star to collapse, it may collapse all the way to a preon star.

**Example trigger:** A collision with a dense object (e.g., a 5 M☉ black hole) at relativistic speeds would have this effect.

## Critical Questions and Verification Tests

### Fundamental Physics Questions

**Q1: Energy Conservation During Collapse**

In standard collapse models, gravitational binding energy is released as neutrinos and gravitational waves, resulting in M_final < M_initial. The pressure-dominated model claims M_obs increases during collapse (2 M☉ NS → 5 M☉ BH) while the system radiates energy.

**Resolution:** The key is distinguishing M_baryonic from M_gravitational. The baryonic mass remains constant (or decreases slightly due to energy radiation). However, M_gravitational = M_baryonic + (binding energy + pressure contribution)/c². In ultra-dense states, the pressure contribution dominates, making M_gravitational >> M_baryonic even as the system loses energy to radiation.

**Critical test:** Energy accounting must show that total energy-momentum is conserved: M_baryonic c² + E_radiation + E_gravitational_waves = constant, while M_gravitational changes due to pressure term evolution.

**Q2: Magnitude of Pressure Contribution**

How large can the pressure term realistically be in the TOV equation? Can it really produce a 2.5× multiplier (2 M☉ baryonic → 5 M☉ gravitational)?

**Answer requires:** Full numerical solution of TOV/Hartle-Thorne equations with realistic quark matter and hypothetical preon matter equations of state. Current calculations suggest pressure contributions of ~10-20% for neutron stars, but extrapolation to quark/preon densities is uncertain.

**Q3: Interior Structure Evidence**

What direct evidence do we have for internal structure inside event horizons? Standard GR assumes empty spacetime inside with central singularity.

**Proposed evidence:**
- GW waveforms during merger show extended emission (spin inside horizon)
- Mass "deficits" in mergers correlate with final spin (pressure reduction)
- No evidence for true singularities (all BH observations consistent with dense matter)

**Open question:** Can GR simulations with realistic matter EOS reproduce observed GW signals?

### Black Hole Hypothesis Explanation

**Chain of reasoning:**

1. **Exponential disk surface density:**
   - Σ(r) ∝ exp(-r/R_d) falls faster than 1/r beyond R_d
   - This is why V_bar declines past ~1.7 kpc
   - Pure 1/r (Mestel disk) would give flat V_circ

2. **Metallicity gradient in disk galaxies:**
   - Metallicity decreases with radius: Z(r) ∝ exp(-r/R_Z)
   - R_Z ≈ 2-3 R_d (gradient scale length)
   - NGC 2976 outer disk: lower metallicity environment

3. **Failed supernova fraction vs metallicity:**
   - Lower metallicity → weaker stellar winds → more massive cores
   - Failed SN fraction: f_fail ∝ (Z/Z☉)^(-α) where α ~ 0.5-1.0
   - At r > R_d, higher fraction of massive stars directly collapse to BH

4. **Pressure-dominated black hole masses:**
   - Standard collapse: M_BH ≈ M_core (baryonic)
   - Pressure-dominated model: M_BH(gravitational) ≈ 2.5 × M_core
   - Each BH contributes 2.5× the gravity of its stellar progenitor

5. **Radial black hole fraction profile:**
   - Inner disk (high Z): f_BH ≈ 5-10% (most stars explode)
   - Outer disk (low Z): f_BH ≈ 20-40% (more direct collapse)
   - f_BH(r) increases with radius

6. **Gravitational enhancement:**
   - ΔM_grav(r) = f_BH(r) × M_stars(r) × (2.5 - 1)
   - ΔM_grav(r) = 1.5 × f_BH(r) × M_stars(r)
   - This adds exactly where V_bar declines

**Result:** Radially-increasing BH fraction with enhanced gravity per object produces additional gravitational pull that keeps V_total flat even as V_bar from visible stars declines.

**Profile match:**
- Small deficit at r ≈ 1 kpc (few BHs, high metallicity)
- Growing deficit at r > 1.7 kpc (more BHs, lower metallicity)
- Matches observed V_obs - V_bar pattern

### The BH Explosion Framework

**Two independent physical processes explain VPOS:**

#### Process 1: Multi-Plane Formation (Standard Angular Momentum Collapse)

**Initial state:** Spherical gas cloud (pressure-supported)

**Collapse sequence:**
1. Gas forms nebulae → stars (pressure-supported sphere)
2. Stars evolve, some go supernova
3. Gas returned to ISM settles to lowest energy state
4. **Different radii can have different angular momentum vectors**
5. Each radius collapses to plane perpendicular to local J

**Result for MW:**
- Inner regions (r < 17 kpc): Collapse to plane A → main disk
- Outer regions (r > 20 kpc): Collapse to plane B → VPOS (orthogonal)
- Gap (17-20 kpc): Void forms (see Process 2)

**Why don't planes merge?**
- No material in gap to transfer angular momentum
- Each plane stable in its own J configuration
- Orthogonal → no overlap → no torques
- **Planes remain distinct indefinitely**

**This is standard astrophysics** - complex angular momentum structure in collapsing gas clouds is well-established.

#### Process 2: Roll-Up Mechanism (Boundary Instability)

**Why does outer plane fragment into discrete dSphs instead of remaining continuous?**

**The boundary instability:**

**In main disk (stable):**
```
Inner stars → SPIRAL ARM ← Outer stars
   (pull)     (density)      (pull)
              enhancement
                  ↓
    Star oscillates through, continues orbit
```

**At disk edge (unstable):**
```
    VOID → DENSITY CLUMP ← Outer edge stars
  (r<17)      (arm)           (pull)
             
              Star gets CAPTURED
```

**Physical mechanism:**

1. **Density perturbation forms** in outer plane (like spiral arm)
2. **Stars from inner edge approach clump**
3. **No restoring force** from inner disk (void!)
4. **Gravitational capture** into orbit around clump
5. **Clump gains mass** → stronger gravity
6. **Positive feedback** → runaway fragmentation
7. **Dwarf spheroidal forms** from captured stars

**Void formation:**
- Stars between clumps pulled into nearest clump
- Inner regions of outer plane depopulated
- Leaves void at r = 17-20 kpc
- Only dense clumps (dSphs) remain at r > 20 kpc

**Timescale:**
- Instability growth: few orbital periods
- At r = 20 kpc: P_orb ~ 500 Myr
- **Roll-up completes in <1 Gyr**
- Happened 10-8 Gyr ago

**This explains:**
- Sparseness (stars stretched during capture)
- Velocity dispersion (captured from different velocities)
- Discrete objects (boundary instability fragments)
- Planar orbits (formed from plane, orbit in plane)
- Void between disk and VPOS (material consumed by clumps)

#### Process 3: Black Hole Formation in dSphs (Stellar Evolution)

**Once dSphs form, stellar evolution proceeds differently than in main disk:**

**Environment factors favoring BH formation:**

1. **Low metallicity** (outer plane formed with [Fe/H] ~ -2)
   - Weaker stellar winds (metal line driving)
   - Stars retain more angular momentum
   - Cores retain more mass

2. **Sparse stellar environment**
   - Minimal tidal interactions
   - Stars evolve in isolation
   - Angular momentum preserved

3. **High spin retention**
   - Weak magnetic braking (low metallicity)
   - Minimal tidal spin-down (isolation)
   - Stars reach core collapse with higher spin

**Spin-cushioned collapse mechanism:**

Standard massive star death:
1. Core collapse
2. **Bounce** (neutron degeneracy pressure)
3. Shockwave ejects envelope
4. **SUPERNOVA**
5. Leaves NS or BH

High-spin star death:
1. Core collapse
2. **No hard bounce** (rotation provides centrifugal support)
3. Core becomes NS (even if M_core < 2 M☉)
4. **Envelope accretes onto NS**
5. NS mass grows: 1.5 → 2.0 → 2.5 M☉
6. **Exceeds TOV limit → collapses to BH**
7. **NO SUPERNOVA** (failed SN)

**Result in dSphs:**
- Failed SN fraction: 70-90% (vs 5-10% in solar neighborhood)
- Most massive stars → BHs
- Minimal metal enrichment (few SNe)
- Metallicity stays at [Fe/H] ~ -2

**Observational support:**
- Failed SNe more common at low metallicity (observed)
- Pulsar mass-period L-shape (massive pulsars don't spin slowly)
- Mass gaps (2.5 M☉ NS → 5 M☉ BH requires pressure enhancement)

#### Process 4: Black Hole Explosions (Compact Object Physics)

**BH accumulation phase (10-6 Gyr ago):**
- BHs form via failed SNe
- Each BH: M_grav ≈ 5 M☉ (2.5× M_baryonic via pressure)
- Accumulate in dSphs
- Minimal gas return to ISM
- Star formation continues with depleting gas

**BH explosion epoch (6-4 Gyr ago):**
- BHs spin down over Gyr timescales
- Spin → centrifugal support → pressure relief
- Slow-spinning BHs: high pressure → high M_grav
- **Critical threshold reached** → catastrophic pressure release
- **BLACK HOLES EXPLODE**

**Explosion effects:**

1. **Gas ejection**
   - Explosion products: pristine H, He (trace Li)
   - Cleared ALL remaining gas from dSphs
   - No gas → no star formation (ceased 4 Gyr ago)

2. **Metallicity dilution**
   - Small amount of metals from rare SNe
   - Diluted by pristine BH explosion products
   - Final [Fe/H] ~ -2 to -2.5 (observed)

3. **Stellar ejection** (some dSphs)
   - Energetic explosions eject stars
   - Creates additional sparseness
   - High velocity dispersion component

4. **BH remnants remain**
   - Not all BHs explode (some still spinning)
   - Remaining BHs provide dark mass
   - M/L ~ 10-1000 (observed)

**Current state (last 4 Gyr):**
- Sparse stellar population (original + roll-up capture - ejected)
- No gas (cleared by explosions)
- No star formation (no fuel)
- High M/L (remaining BHs with 2.5× enhancement)
- Low metallicity (never enriched, diluted by pristine ejecta)

### Why This Requires Non-Singular Black Holes

**This is independent evidence** beyond:

### Elliptical Galaxies: Multiple Plane Systems

**Observation:** Elliptical galaxies are triaxial, pressure-supported, no coherent rotation

**Hypothesis:** Ellipticals formed from multiple orthogonal planes at ALL radii

**Scenario:**

1. **Initial collapse with complex angular momentum**
   - Different radii → different J vectors
   - Forms multiple planes:
     - r = 0-5 kpc → Plane A (θ₁, φ₁)
     - r = 5-10 kpc → Plane B (θ₂, φ₂)
     - r = 10-15 kpc → Plane C (θ₃, φ₃)
     - etc.

2. **Each plane has sharp inner boundary**
   - Boundary instability at each edge
   - Roll-up fragmentation at smaller radii
   - Shorter orbital periods → faster fragmentation

3. **Fragments merge via dynamical friction**
   - At r = 5 kpc: t_merge ~ 100 Myr (short!)
   - At r = 20 kpc: t_merge ~ 5 Gyr (long)
   - Inner fragments merge quickly
   - Outer fragments remain distinct (like VPOS)

4. **Result: Pressure-supported elliptical**
   - Merged inner fragments → churned, triaxial core
   - Multiple velocity components
   - High velocity dispersion
   - No coherent rotation
   - Possible outer shell structure (if fragments survive)

**Why ellipticals differ from spirals:**

- **Spirals:** Single dominant plane + outer orthogonal plane
  - Main disk stable (no roll-up)
  - VPOS fragments remain distinct

- **Ellipticals:** Multiple planes at ALL radii
  - All boundaries unstable (roll-up everywhere)
  - Inner fragments merge (short t_merge)
  - Creates triaxial, dispersion-dominated system

**Observational support:**

1. **Shell structures in ellipticals**
   - Common in 20-40% of ellipticals
   - Interpreted as merger debris
   - Alternative: Roll-up fragments that didn't merge

2. **Kinematically distinct cores (KDCs)**
   - Counter-rotating cores in some ellipticals
   - Standard: Merger remnants
   - Alternative: Preserved plane from multi-plane collapse

3. **Globular cluster systems**
   - Ellipticals have 100s-1000s of GCs (vs ~150 in MW)
   - Often bimodal metallicity distribution
   - Interpretation: Dense fragments from roll-up at different epochs
   - GC ages 4-10 Gyr (matches BH explosion timescale)

4. **No gas in ellipticals**
   - Standard: Stripped by mergers
   - Alternative: Multiple BH explosion epochs cleared gas

**This is speculative** but provides unified framework:
- Spirals: One main plane (stable) + outer plane (fragments)
- Ellipticals: Multiple planes (all fragment and merge)
- S0 galaxies: Intermediate (partial fragmentation)
